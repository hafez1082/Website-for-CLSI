CLSI's History


How it all Started



In the 1960s, during a time of technological revolution of clinical laboratory products , the US Centers for Disease Control announced the need to form a national committee on clinical laboratory Standards. NCCLS , or the National Committee for Clinical Laboratory Standards was then formed.







In fact, "36 representatives of 19 scinetific and professional groups and governmental agencies" gathered and agreed to form it in order to standardize blood chemistry testing, as mentioned in NCCLS's official news release.
"Preparation of Manuals for Installation, Operation, and Repair of Laboratory Instruments," was their first standard, which was released in 1969, a year after their formal organization .

Twenty laboratory standards were written over NCCLS' first 10 years.





NCCLS and ANSI



In 1977 , 8 years after its creation, NCCLS was accredited by the American National Standards Institute (ANSI).
More than 80 standards had been written by 1985, more than 130 standards projects had been completed, and over 1,000 clinical laboratory scientists had participated in NCCLS standards initiatives.





NCCLS and ISO



In 1997, NCCLS proposed and created the International Organization for Standardization's Technical Committee 212 (ISO/TC 212).





CLSI, The New NCCLS



In 2005, NCCLS became the Clinical and Laboratory Standards Institute (CLSI),and Global Health Partnerships was formed. Global Health Partnerships programme provides resource-constrained laboratories with evaluation services, training, education, and technical assistance to help them apply CLSI standards, improve quality, and prepare for international accreditation.





CLSI and the FDA



Today, “the FDA recognizes seven CLSI standards, either in whole or part, which contain standardized reference methods that measure the susceptibility of bacteria and fungi to antimicrobial agents”.
